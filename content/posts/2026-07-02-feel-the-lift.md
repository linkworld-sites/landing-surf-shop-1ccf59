There's a moment that happens about 12 minutes into most people's first eFoil session.

The board starts to vibrate differently. The drag pulls back. And then — quietly, without fanfare — the hull lifts clear of the water's surface and you are *flying*.

Not metaphorically. Not "feels like flying." Actually flying. Suspended above the water on a carbon wing, the chop and noise of the surface disappearing beneath you. The only sound left is the hum of the motor and whatever sound your heart makes when it forgets to beat for half a second.

That moment is what we're here for.

## What People Get Wrong Before They Arrive

Most first-timers expect it to be like surfing. It isn't. Surfing requires wave timing, paddle fitness, and a willingness to get worked by the ocean until you find your rhythm. eFoiling requires almost none of that.

The board is stable. The motor is smooth and predictable. You control speed with a handheld remote that's about as complicated as a TV volume button. Your job isn't to fight the water — it's to relax into it.

The riders who struggle most in the first session aren't the ones who've never surfed. They're often the ones who *have* surfed, because they keep trying to compensate for a wave that isn't there.

**The secret:** stand tall, keep your hips loose, and trust the foil to do the work.

## What to Actually Expect

**Minutes 0–5:** Lying prone on the board, getting a feel for the motor's power band. Your instructor is in the water beside you (or on a follow boat). The board is barely moving. You feel stupid. This is normal.

**Minutes 5–15:** On your knees, building confidence with the remote. The board starts to plane — not foiling yet, just skimming fast. It feels surprisingly good. You stand up. You fall. You stand again.

**Minutes 15–25:** First attempts at standing. This is where most people find their "oh" moment. The board steadies. The foil begins to engage. You feel the lift before you see it.

**Minutes 25–30 (Express session) or further:** You're flying. You're adjusting your weight, leaning into turns, trying to figure out how fast you can push it before your instructor waves you down.

By the end, you'll be annoyed the session is over.

A stiff, responsive foil setup teaches you muscle memory that transfers directly if you ever buy your own board. Cheap gear teaches you to compensate for cheap gear.
